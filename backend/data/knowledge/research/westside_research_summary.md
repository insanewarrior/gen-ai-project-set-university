# Westside Barbell and EliteFTS: Conjugate Method Research Summary

## The Conjugate System Diverges from Linear Periodization

The Conjugate Method, popularized by Louie Simmons of Westside Barbell and extensively documented by Dave Tate through EliteFTS, represents a divergent but successful methodology focused primarily on maximal strength and power development for geared and raw powerlifting and sports performance. Unlike traditional linear periodization models, the Conjugate system runs multiple physical adaptations concurrently, attempting to improve speed, maximal strength, and hypertrophy within the same microcycle.

This concurrency is the system's foundational claim: phases of dedicated speed work, dedicated strength work, and dedicated hypertrophy work each lose their gains when the phase ends. Running all three together prevents this regression at the cost of programming complexity.

## The Four-Day Training Split

The mathematical structure relies on a strict four-day split: two Maximal Effort (ME) days and two Dynamic Effort (DE) days, separated by 72 hours each for the same lift family.

Maximal Effort Days: trainees work up to a 1-3 repetition maximum on a variation of the main lift (e.g., box squat, rack press, suspended good morning, reverse band deadlift). These variations must rotate every 1-3 weeks to prevent the central nervous system from accommodating to the movement pattern. The ME work is immediately followed by high-volume accessory work specifically targeting the athlete's weak muscular links.

Dynamic Effort Days: this phase utilizes submaximal loads — typically 50% to 75% of 1RM — moved at maximal concentric velocities. This is paired with accommodating resistance (heavy bands and chains) to alter the strength curve, accommodate the mechanical advantage at lockout, and teach compensatory acceleration.

## EliteFTS Documentation

EliteFTS provides extensive resources detailing the methodology. "The Vault" is a comprehensive 200-page manual compiling a decade of PR-shattering training tips, covering Bench Tips, Squat Tips, Deadlift Tips, a Pro Bench Specialist Program, the S4 Winter program, the Force Training Program, and the "Live-Learn and Pass On" philosophy.

EliteFTS outlines "The 8 Keys to Maximal Strength Development": coaching, teamwork, conditioning, strength, speed, recovery, attitude, and nutrition. The underlying logic instructs athletes that weaknesses dictate exercise selection. If an athlete struggles with core stability, the program algorithm must prescribe specific trunk-loading movements rather than simply increasing overall squat volume — embodying the philosophy of "doing the stuff you suck at" to bridge performance gaps.

## Why The CNS Limit Drives The System Architecture

Training above 90 percent of 1RM for more than 2-3 weeks degrades the central nervous system. Linear periodization runs into this wall in late-cycle peaking phases and produces the characteristic pattern of strong gym lifts followed by missed competition attempts. The Conjugate Method works around the wall by rotating the max-effort movement weekly or biweekly, treating the new variation as a new lift with its own 1RM and avoiding the chronic 90-percent-plus loading on any single pattern.

This rotation is the system's most important practical innovation. Without it, the same volume of max-effort work would degrade the lifter just as linear programs do. With it, max effort can be performed weekly indefinitely.

## Accommodating Resistance Mathematics

For dynamic effort (speed-strength) work, the standard ratio is 65 percent barbell weight to 35 percent band tension. For strength-speed (slow strength used with maximal weights), the ratio inverts to 65 percent band tension and 35 percent barbell weight. These ratios produce the desired bar speed for each adaptation type.

Chains accommodate similarly to bands but with a different curve. Chain weight increases linearly as the bar rises (chain links lift off the floor); band weight increases nonlinearly (bands stretch and accumulate force). Chains are more forgiving and are used for longer training cycles; bands are more aggressive and are limited to 3-4 weeks before swapping back to chains to prevent excessive joint stress.

## The Weak-Link Programming Logic

Once max effort and dynamic effort handle the primary lifts, the remaining programming budget goes to special exercises chosen based on the athlete's specific weaknesses. The catalog is large: reverse hyperextensions, glute-ham raises, pull-throughs, sled drags, dimel deadlifts, JM presses, board presses, banded triceps work, lateral raises, abdominal work.

The programming heuristic: identify where the lifter misses, what muscle group is weak relative to others, and program 2-4 special exercises per session that target that weakness. Rotate exercises every 2-4 weeks as their effectiveness diminishes. This is opposite to the conventional advice of "stick with the basics" — Westside argues that the basics are already covered by the ME and DE work, and the special exercises are where individualization actually happens.

## Career Longevity as a Diagnostic

A meaningful indicator of system efficacy: most American powerlifters of the 1970s and 1980s lasted 5-6 years before chronic injuries forced retirement. Westside members have competed for decades. The conjugate method's rotation, weak-link work, and avoidance of chronic 80-90 percent loading on the competition lifts appears to produce dramatically lower injury rates and longer careers, which is a quietly important benefit beyond the maximum totals achieved.
