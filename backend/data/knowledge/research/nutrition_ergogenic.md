# Nutrition and Ergogenic Aids for Strength Athletes

## Protein Intake for Strength and Hypertrophy

The International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) position stand dispels outdated clinical fears regarding high protein intake in healthy individuals. Concerns about renal stress in populations with normal kidney function are unfounded. Healthy kidneys handle dietary protein loads in the strength-athlete range without adverse adaptation.

The empirical data establishes that exercising individuals require between 1.4 and 2.0 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day to optimize muscle protein synthesis (MPS). For nutritional models or dietary recommendations within a strength application, this mathematical bracket serves as the immutable bounding constraint. Below 1.4 g/kg, MPS falls short of supporting adaptation; above 2.0 g/kg, additional protein produces no further benefit and displaces other macronutrients.

Athletes in caloric deficit (cutting phase) benefit from the upper end of this range (1.8–2.4 g/kg) because higher protein preserves lean mass under restricted energy availability. Athletes in caloric surplus can sit comfortably in the middle of the range.

## Protein Quality and Leucine

Not all protein sources yield equal physiological responses. High-quality sources rich in essential amino acids — particularly the branched-chain amino acid leucine — are critical for triggering the mTOR pathway, the primary biological driver of MPS.

Whey, dairy, eggs, and lean animal meats are leucine-dense (typically 8–11% leucine by amino acid content). Plant proteins generally have lower leucine density (5–7%), so vegan athletes need slightly higher total protein intake or strategic combinations (rice and pea, soy and others) to match the leucine threshold per meal.

The leucine threshold for triggering MPS in a single meal is approximately 2.5–3 grams of leucine, equivalent to 25–35 g of high-quality protein for most athletes. This frames meal frequency: 4–5 protein-containing meals per day, each crossing the leucine threshold, optimize MPS more effectively than 2 large meals or 8 small ones.

## Peri-Workout Nutrition

The timing of protein intake encompassing the exercise window facilitates decreases in protein degradation while simultaneously aiding cellular repair and promoting greater gains in fat-free mass. The classical "anabolic window" was overstated, but a rough rule holds: eat a protein-containing meal within 1–2 hours before training and 1–2 hours after, and the timing nuances above and beyond that have small effects.

Carbohydrate intake around training matters more for athletes performing high-volume sessions than for athletes performing short heavy sessions. A heavy max-effort triple uses very little glycogen; a 10-rep squat marathon uses substantially more.

## Creatine Monohydrate

Creatine monohydrate is explicitly categorized by the ISSN as the most effective, safe, and legal ergogenic nutritional supplement currently available to athletes. The position stand justifies standard dosing protocols: a loading phase of 20 g/day for 5–7 days followed by a maintenance dose of 3–5 g/day. The loading phase is optional — daily 5 g produces full saturation in 2–3 weeks without the loading. Both protocols reach the same equilibrium creatine concentration in muscle.

The physiological mechanism involves the rapid regeneration of adenosine triphosphate (ATP) during the alactic anaerobic energy pathway. Creatine phosphate donates a phosphate group to ADP, regenerating ATP within seconds — exactly the energy demand of a max-effort lift or short sprint. Increased intracellular phosphocreatine stores improve high-intensity exercise capacity, facilitate greater total training volume, and augment maximal strength output and power generation during repeated bouts of explosive activity.

Creatine is well-studied in pediatric, adult, and elderly populations. The safety profile is established at the doses used by athletes; concerns about kidney stress in healthy individuals are unsupported by the empirical literature. Creatine has additional benefits beyond strength: improved cognitive function under sleep deprivation, reduced fatigue during repeated sprints, and possibly neuroprotective effects in aging populations.

For practical recommendation: 5 g of creatine monohydrate daily, taken any time of day with a meal containing carbohydrates (which slightly improves uptake via insulin response). Brand and form do not matter beyond purity — monohydrate is the most-studied form, and other forms (HCl, ethyl ester, buffered) have not demonstrated superior outcomes despite their marketing premium.

## Other Supplements: Effective vs Marketed

Beta-alanine has modest evidence for high-intensity exercise lasting 1–4 minutes. Caffeine has strong evidence for both endurance and high-intensity performance at 3–6 mg/kg taken 30–60 minutes pre-workout. Beetroot juice (nitrate) shows benefit for endurance under specific conditions. These three supplements have ISSN-grade evidence behind them.

The vast majority of supplements marketed to strength athletes — testosterone boosters, BCAAs (in athletes consuming adequate protein), specific amino acids, exotic herbs — have either no evidence or weak evidence relative to creatine and caffeine. A capstone strength application should not promote these without flagging the evidence gap.

## Hydration

Hydration affects strength output linearly once an athlete reaches 2 percent or greater body mass loss from sweat. Below that threshold, hydration status has small effects on strength performance. Practical implication: drink to thirst plus a bit more on training days, weigh yourself before and after long sessions to estimate sweat loss, and replace fluids over the following 24 hours rather than chugging during the session.

For powerlifting meets and cutting weight, the rules change — but cutting protocols are sport-specific and should be guided by experienced coaches, not generic application advice.
