# Stronger by Science Research-Based Programming

## Methodology and Sources

Stronger by Science, spearheaded by Greg Nuckols, is characterized by rigorous adherence to empirical evidence and systematic meta-analyses. The platform has published extensive literature on training volume, the minimum effective training dose, and biomechanical leverage.

Research provided by SBS includes doctoral-level studies indicating the absolute minimal amount of training a trained powerlifter must undertake to increase maximal strength, highlighting highly efficient single-set protocols for time-poor athletes. The minimum effective dose research is particularly important: it shows that for trained powerlifters, even very low volumes can maintain or modestly improve strength, which has practical implications for injured athletes, busy professionals, and the elderly.

## The 28 Free Programs

The "28 Free Programs" matrix is widely used across the strength community. The programs vary along three independent dimensions: training frequency (1×, 2×, or 3× per week), experience level (beginner, intermediate, advanced), and competition lift (squat, bench press, deadlift independently). The matrix provides exact percentage-based progressions and periodization schemes for each combination.

The beginner 2× per week squat program requires the athlete to squat at or below 85% of their 1RM on the first day. On the second day they modulate intensity based on daily readiness, lifting anywhere from an 8RM to a 3RM until a new 1RM is tested at the end of the four-week cycle. This auto-regulated approach respects daily variation in capacity that linear programs ignore.

Intermediate variants introduce specific exercise variations to address muscular weaknesses. Front squats are added for quad-dominant lifters whose squat depth or speed out of the hole reflects insufficient quad strength. The variation choice is driven by diagnostic logic rather than by general program rotation.

## Hormones and Training

The SBS corpus includes in-depth analysis of the chronic effects of resistance training on hormone levels. Acute spikes in testosterone and growth hormone after a workout do not predict long-term hypertrophy or strength gains, contradicting popular bodybuilding lore. Long-term baseline hormone levels matter; acute spikes do not.

High-volume training elevates cortisol relative to testosterone, but this elevation is transient and self-correcting in well-recovered athletes. Cortisol-to-testosterone ratio panic is largely unjustified for athletes managing fatigue properly through volume landmarks, sleep, and nutrition.

## Biomechanics of Equipment

SBS research includes the biomechanical effects of weightlifting shoes with elevated heels. The elevated heel improves knee flexion angles during leg presses and squats, allowing greater quad recruitment and depth without the ankle mobility that flat shoes require. For athletes with limited ankle dorsiflexion, shoes with 0.75-inch heels can be the difference between a productive squat and one that compromises depth or torso angle.

Belt usage, knee wraps, and other equipment have similarly been studied for their actual mechanical contribution rather than dismissed as "cheating" or accepted as universally beneficial. The data supports selective, athlete-specific equipment choices rather than blanket prescriptions.

## The Minimum Effective Dose Insight

The most actionable finding from SBS research: for trained powerlifters, very low volumes (a single hard set per lift, twice per week) can maintain strength indefinitely and produce small gains over time. This reframes how we should think about busy or injured athletes. They do not need to abandon training when life prevents the high volumes they used to do — a minimum-effective-dose program preserves much of what they have built.

For coaches, this insight changes the deload conversation. A traditional deload reduces both volume and intensity by 30–50 percent for a week. The minimum-effective-dose data suggests that volume can drop more aggressively (60–80 percent) while intensity stays high, producing better recovery without losing the high-intensity neural training that maintains strength.
