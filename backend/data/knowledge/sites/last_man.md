# LastManStanding.org Coaching Resource

## Site Scope

last-man.org is the original publication site of the LMS coaching team — Dmitry Bondarenko, Vadim Myakotin, Andrey Barskov, Dmitry Golovinsky, and Vladimir Nichik. The site predates the dedicated cycle distribution platform at lms-plans.site and contains the methodological articles that explain the science behind the cycles.

Primary categories include Bodybuilding (Culturism), Strength Training, Thoughts and Discussions, and Training Cycles. Strength Training is further subdivided into Arm Wrestling, Fundamentals, Women's Training, Bench Press, and Powerlifting. The companion tool plan.last-man.org is a web-based training management interface used by athletes following the cycles.

## Methodology Articles: Periodization

The article "Power Cycles and Periodization Revisited" examines the organization of strength periods in powerlifting, distinguishing between powerlifting and weightlifting in their periodization needs. Powerlifting peaks are typically longer than weightlifting peaks because the demanded movements (squat, bench, deadlift) recover more slowly than the snatch and clean-and-jerk. The article emphasizes the 4–6 rep range as the sweet spot for strength development in the strength period, citing classical Soviet theorists A.N. Vorobyev and the Olympic weightlifting champion Vasily Alekseyev.

"Power Cycles: Overview of Training Layouts" details the total rep count calculations and describes typical training layouts for strength periods. The periodization structure is segmented into strength phases, peak phases, and speed development phases, with layouts categorized by complexity (simple, medium, complex) and intensity (light, heavy versions). Each layout is mapped to a specific phase and athlete level.

"Squats and Deadlift: Muscle Function" analyzes muscle group activation patterns and explains static versus dynamic loading concepts. The article distinguishes between exercises that load muscles concentrically (deadlift, squat ascent) versus those that load eccentrically with significant time-under-tension (slow descent squats, paused movements).

## Methodology Articles: Bench Press

The bench press category emphasizes proper technique development as foundational. Correct technique allows the athlete to press with greater power and speed. Key technical elements: scapular retraction and leg drive engagement as core stability components, bar path correction (asymmetrical bar movement leads to asymmetrical muscle development), and counter-movement technique where advanced lifters use body acceleration off the chest to improve power output.

Programming for bench press is organized into periodized phases. Strength development periods focus on heavy loads (70–90% of max) with carefully regulated lift quantity for intensity adaptation. Speed-focused phases use lower loads with explosive execution to develop rate of force development. Endurance blocks shift to higher repetition work addressing muscular stamina and adaptation capacity. Peak phases pre-competition emphasize maximal load acclimation.

Accessory exercise selection is categorized by purpose. Special exercises target weak points such as the lockout, bottom position, or stability. Competition-specific variations mimic competition demands with bench shirts and other equipment. General development work supports overall muscle balance and injury prevention.

## Methodology Articles: Powerlifting

"Dynamics of Bar Lifting in Deadlift With and Without Bouncing" analyzes deadlift execution with and without floor bounce, examining how external and internal forces affect bar trajectory across different planes of motion. The dynamics differ meaningfully — bouncing produces a momentary load reduction but compromises the standardized rep that competition rules require.

"Powerlifting: Microcycle Organization" addresses the weekly cycle structure required when training all three lifts. Strategic load distribution and recovery management matter because the lifts share muscular demands (lower-back loading from squat and deadlift, shoulder loading from bench and bar racking). The microcycle must distribute these stressors so that recovery for one lift does not interfere with another.

Periodization and planning content covers training period structure, intensity management, and load adjustment based on organizational factors and stress responses. Endurance capacity development articles examine volume and intensity parameters for building strength-endurance across the three lifts.

## Methodology Articles: Women's Training

The site includes content specific to female athletes covering load dosing across menstrual cycle phases. The follicular phase typically allows higher absolute loads and shorter recovery; the luteal phase requires reduced volumes or slightly lower intensity. Recovery volume considerations specific to women include the relative bone-mineral-density vulnerability and the somewhat higher injury rates in certain joints.

Organizational principles for female athletes acknowledge that most existing strength programming was developed and tested on male athletes; the site recommends adapting volume and frequency landmarks based on individual response rather than blanket scaling.

## The Soviet School Foundation

A consistent theme across the methodology articles is grounding in Soviet sports science. Special work capacity development (special physical preparation), general physical preparation, tonnage tracking (total rep count), and relative intensity calculation by Chernyak's method are all imported directly from the Soviet weightlifting and powerlifting tradition rather than from American or Western European frameworks. This makes the LMS system distinctive in English-speaking strength communities, where most programming descends from a different lineage (Sheiko, Smolov, JTS, Westside, RP).

The practical implication: an athlete fluent in Western percentage-based programming and RPE will encounter unfamiliar terminology when reading LMS materials. Total rep count is similar to "total volume" but is counted at the lift level. Relative intensity by Chernyak is similar to RPE but is computed mathematically from average bar weight rather than estimated subjectively. The concepts map but do not align one-to-one.
