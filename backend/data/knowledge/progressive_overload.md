# Progressive Overload and Load Management

## The Progressive Overload Principle

Progressive overload is the foundational driver of strength adaptation: the training stimulus must increase over time to continue producing gains. Without it, the body reaches homeostasis and adaptation ceases. Overload can be applied through: increasing load (weight), increasing volume (sets × reps), increasing frequency, decreasing rest intervals, or improving movement quality. For strength athletes, load and volume are the primary levers.

## The SRA Curve (Stimulus-Recovery-Adaptation)

The SRA (Stimulus-Recovery-Adaptation) cycle describes how the body responds to training:

1. **Stimulus**: Training causes fatigue and a temporary decrease in performance
2. **Recovery**: The body repairs damaged tissue over 24-72 hours
3. **Adaptation (supercompensation)**: Performance rises above the pre-training baseline for a window of 48-96 hours
4. **Return to baseline**: If no new stimulus is applied, adaptation dissipates

Programming must time the next training session to hit during the supercompensation window. Training too soon causes chronic fatigue accumulation; training too late allows detraining. For heavy compound lifts, 48-72 hours between sessions targeting the same movement is typical.

## Prilepin's Chart

Prilepin's Chart, derived from Soviet weightlifting research, provides empirically derived rep ranges for each intensity zone to maximize strength adaptation without excessive fatigue:

| Intensity (% 1RM) | Reps per Set | Optimal Total Reps | Acceptable Range |
|---|---|---|---|
| 55-65% | 3-6 | 24 | 18-30 |
| 70-79% | 3-6 | 18 | 12-24 |
| 80-89% | 2-4 | 15 | 10-20 |
| 90%+ | 1-2 | 7 | 4-10 |

Using more than the upper range at high intensities accumulates fatigue faster than adaptation occurs. Using less than the lower range at moderate intensities provides insufficient stimulus. These guidelines apply primarily to competition-style compound lifts (squat, bench, deadlift, clean, snatch).

## Fatigue Management

Accumulated fatigue masks fitness. An athlete may be stronger than they feel during a high-volume block because fatigue suppresses performance expression. Deload weeks (every 3-4 weeks) reduce volume by 40-50% to dissipate fatigue and reveal true fitness levels.

Signs of excessive fatigue accumulation: persistent joint soreness, declining motivation, sleep disruption, performance plateaus across 2+ sessions. When these appear, a deload is warranted regardless of the planned schedule.

## Load Progression Schemes

- **Linear progression**: Add 2.5-5kg per session (novice-appropriate, unsustainable beyond 8-12 weeks)
- **Double progression**: Progress reps within a range before increasing load (e.g., 3x8-12; when 3x12 is reached, add 5% load and return to 3x8)
- **Percentage-based progression**: Add 2-5% load each week within a mesocycle; reset intensity at the start of each new block
- **Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE)-based**: Target a specific RPE (e.g., RPE 8 = 2 reps in reserve); load auto-adjusts to daily readiness
