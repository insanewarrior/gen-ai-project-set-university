# Embedded Cycles for Standing Press Training

## Overview

The embedded cycles for standing press are self-calculating Excel programs designed to integrate standing press (military press) training into an athlete's existing program without disrupting their primary specialization. The "embedded" terminology distinguishes these cycles from standalone programs — they attach to whatever the athlete is already doing for their primary sport (powerlifting, bench specialization, bodybuilding) and add structured standing press progression on top.

## Three Difficulty Levels

The cycles ship in three level-specific variants. MPBeginner.xlsm is for athletes who have never trained the standing press before, emphasizing technique mastery and initial strength gains. MPIntermediate.xlsm is for athletes with consistent standing press experience seeking measurable progression with higher loading appropriate for athletes prioritizing the lift. MPExperienced.xlsm is for advanced athletes — the documentation explicitly notes it is not recommended if standing press performance is less than 110 kilograms, which is the threshold below which the cycle's load math produces underwhelming progress relative to the athlete's actual capacity.

## Setup for Beginners

The beginner variant requires careful 1RM input. Athletes should conservatively estimate their 1-rep max and add 5 to 7 kilograms to the starting value the cycle uses. This counter-intuitive instruction reflects how the cycle is structured: it begins below the athlete's true capacity to allow technical mastery during early sessions, then progresses to and beyond the true 1RM as confidence and form solidify. Underestimating the starting 1RM gives the cycle headroom; overestimating produces loads the athlete fails before learning the lift properly.

The intermediate and advanced variants do not use this offset; the athlete inputs their actual 1RM directly because they already have technical proficiency.

## Integration with Primary Training

The cycles attach at the end of training sessions after primary movements. This placement matters: standing press at the start of a session would compete with the primary lifts for nervous-system resources; placing it at the end allows the athlete to bring full attention to their main work first.

Key integration restrictions. Athletes cannot perform additional shoulder exercises during the same workout. Standing press variations (push press, jerks, behind-the-neck press) are prohibited during the embedded cycle's run. Rotator cuff work is recommended as a complementary practice — small low-load rotator cuff work supports shoulder health under the increased press volume. Bench press variations remain optional and depend on the athlete's primary cycle.

## Training Structure

The embedded cycles use 3 to 4 training days per week. Recommended schedule patterns: Monday-Wednesday-Friday (3 days) or Tuesday-Thursday-Saturday. Athletes whose primary cycle uses 4 sessions per week can integrate the embedded cycle on those same days.

Load management uses a 0.5% adjustment factor (correction percentage) modifiable weekly. The athlete adjusts the percentage based on subjective recovery and observed difficulty during the prior week.

## Training Principles

The approach emphasizes sub-maximal loads, making the embedded cycles suitable for off-season or competition prep phases when the athlete's primary training does not demand peak intensity from the entire body. Recovery period before testing the standing press 1RM after cycle completion is 4 to 6 days — this gap allows accumulated fatigue to dissipate before the test attempt.

## Why Embedded Rather Than Standalone

Standing press is rarely the primary competitive lift for the LMS catalog's audience (powerlifters, benchers, bodybuilders). Building it as a primary cycle would require dedicating all four training days to its development, displacing the primary specialization. The embedded format respects the athlete's primary goals while adding structured progression for a secondary lift, which produces gradual standing press strength gains without compromising main-lift training.

## Limitations

The embedded cycles work as designed when the athlete's primary training is moderate enough to leave recovery resources for the additional shoulder work. An athlete already at or above their recovery capacity from the primary cycle cannot add an embedded cycle without overload — the cumulative volume exceeds what the body can absorb. The signs of insufficient recovery are predictable: stalled standing press progress, declining bench press numbers (because shoulders share recovery resources), or general fatigue symptoms.

Athletes encountering these signals should pause the embedded cycle and resume after primary training intensity decreases (e.g., during off-season or after a competition).

## When Standing Press Should Be Primary Instead

Some athletes specialize in standing press as their primary lift — federations exist that contest standing press as a competitive event. For these athletes, the embedded format is wrong. They need a primary cycle for the lift, structured similarly to SRC5 or SRC6 but with standing press in the central role and bench press as background. The LMS catalog does not currently publish a dedicated standing press primary cycle, so these athletes typically work with custom programming based on the embedded cycle's structure expanded to four-day-per-week primary training.
