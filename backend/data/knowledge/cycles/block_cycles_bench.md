# Block Self-Calculating Cycles for Bench Press Athletes

## Overview

The block cycles for bench press are 4-week training blocks targeting strength peaks rather than 12-week macrocycles. They function as direct strength-building blocks that can be repeated until progress plateaus, with each successful completion serving as a foundation for the next iteration. The block format is the LMS catalog's primary tool for short-term peaking and for athletes who prefer shorter commitment windows than the standard 12-week cycles require.

## Three Variants by Athlete Level

The block cycles ship in three level-specific variants. Beginners up through II rank use IntenseBeginner.xlsm. Intermediate athletes from I rank through Master of Sport use IntenseIntermediate.xlsm. Advanced athletes at Master of Sport and above use IntenseExperienced.xlsm. The naming convention emphasizes the intensive load organization shared across the variants — all three are heavy-load blocks rather than endurance or hypertrophy blocks.

The variant selection matters because the absolute intensities and recovery demands differ substantially. A beginner running IntenseExperienced will encounter loads their body cannot recover from at the prescribed frequency. An advanced lifter running IntenseBeginner will not get enough stimulus to drive further adaptation.

## Athlete Requirements

Body weight minimum 60 kg with normal proportions. No acute injuries or significant musculoskeletal issues. Adequate recovery capacity and lifestyle compatibility — the cycles are explicitly incompatible with high-stress jobs because the intensive loading consumes recovery resources that physically demanding work would otherwise claim.

The beginner variant includes squats and deadlifts as background lower-body work to maintain those patterns during the bench-focused block. The intermediate and advanced variants include only squats — deadlift maintenance becomes the athlete's responsibility because the higher absolute intensities at those levels would not allow programmed deadlift work without compromising bench recovery.

## Training Structure

Athletes typically train 3 to 5 days weekly during the block, with remaining days avoiding all weighted activity to support recovery. Recommended schedule patterns distribute sessions evenly across the week. For 3-day weeks: Monday-Wednesday-Friday. For 5-day weeks: Monday-Tuesday-Wednesday-Friday-Saturday or similar evenly-distributed patterns.

The intensive load organization means warm-up, working sets, and any accessory work all stay within the strength-period rep cap of six repetitions per set. Higher rep ranges are not used because the block's purpose is peak strength, not muscular endurance or hypertrophy.

## Programming Parameters

The block uses a correction percentage adjustment with weekly modification capability. The athlete can adjust loading by ±2 percentage points based on perceived difficulty during the prior week. This responsive correction allows the block to adapt to the athlete's actual recovery curve rather than rigidly progressing through a predefined load wave that may not match individual response.

## Why Blocks Rather Than 12-Week Cycles

The 12-week cycles are appropriate for athletes building strength from a base. The 4-week blocks are appropriate for three different scenarios. First, peaking — an athlete approaching competition can run a block as the final tune-up rather than a 12-week cycle. Second, athletes who have plateaued on 12-week cycles may benefit from the more aggressive short-term loading of a block. Third, athletes who cannot commit 12 weeks to a single program due to schedule constraints can use blocks as their primary training framework, repeating them as long as progress continues.

## Iterative Use

The block format is designed for iteration. After completing a block successfully, the athlete updates their 1RM inputs and runs the same block again. Strength continues to build as long as the input 1RM rises after each block. Progression typically slows after several iterations as the athlete approaches their genetic ceiling or runs into recovery limits; at that point, switching to a different cycle (12-week strength, endurance, or a different block variant) reintroduces variability and re-enables progress.

## Setup Notes

Athletes should avoid competitions during a block. The 4-week duration is short enough that competition timing usually allows full block completion before competition prep, but running a block that overlaps with a competition compromises both the block's adaptation and the competition performance.

Maintain realistic 1RM inputs. Inflated 1RM values produce loads the athlete cannot complete; deflated values produce undertraining. Adjust loading via the correction percentage rather than by modifying the prescribed exercises.

## Distinction from Block Cycles for Powerlifters

The bench block cycles target bench specialists who treat squat and deadlift as background maintenance. The powerlifting block cycles (separate catalog) target three-lift competitors who need balanced peaking across squat, bench, and deadlift. Choosing the right block format matters — running the bench block on a powerlifter compromises squat and deadlift; running the powerlifting block on a bench specialist dilutes bench-specific stimulus.
