# Block Self-Calculating Cycles for Powerlifters

## Overview

The block cycles for powerlifters are 4-week training programs designed to peak strength across all three competition lifts (squat, bench press, deadlift). Like the block cycles for bench specialists, they function as periods aimed at reaching peak strength and directly increasing the athlete's strength results, distinct from the longer 12-week cycles that build base strength from a lower starting point.

## Three Variants by Athlete Level

The cycles accommodate three skill levels with separate Excel files. Beginners through II-class athletes use the beginner variant. I-class through Master of Sport use the intermediate variant. Advanced competitors above Master of Sport use the advanced variant. As with the bench block cycles, choosing the wrong level variant produces predictable failure modes — the absolute intensities and recovery requirements differ enough across levels that one variant cannot serve all athletes.

## Athlete Requirements

Minimum body weight 60 kg with normal proportions. Established exercise technique and movement patterns in all three lifts. The cycles do not budget time for major technical work; minor refinements during execution are acceptable but the foundational technique must be in place at the start.

No acute injuries or significant musculoskeletal issues. Active lifestyle without excessive occupational stress — the intensive loading combined with three-lift coverage makes the block more demanding than single-lift cycles, requiring proportionally more recovery capacity.

## Training Approach

The cycles employ intensive load organization schemes with selective inclusion of moderate-intensity exercises to maintain technical quality and joint health. Sessions typically involve 3 to 5 weekly workouts distributed evenly across seven days, with general physical preparation exercises during cool-down to support overall conditioning.

The block's intensive structure means that all three lifts receive heavy work within a single 4-week microcycle, which differs from the longer 12-week cycles that may emphasize different lifts in different phases. The compression of three-lift heavy work into 4 weeks demands tight execution and aggressive recovery management.

## Programming Parameters

Athletes input realistic 1RM values for all three lifts and adjust training intensity via a correction percentage rather than modifying the prescribed exercises themselves. This is the LMS philosophy across all cycles: the prescribed exercise selection and structure are validated; deviation should happen at the load-progression knob rather than at the structural level.

The correction percentage adjusts weekly based on observed recovery and progression. Default starting values are calibrated to natural athletes; athletes with pharmacological support apply standard LMS adjustments upward.

## Implementation Notes

Athletes should avoid competitions during the block. The 4-week duration is calibrated to produce peak readiness at the end of the block, so timing the block to end the week before a competition is the optimal pattern. Running a block that ends two or three weeks before a competition risks decay of the peak; running one that overlaps with a competition compromises both.

Maintain realistic 1RM inputs. The published guidance is explicit on this — inflated values produce loads the athlete fails; deflated values produce undertraining. The block's compressed structure makes input accuracy more important than in the 12-week cycles, where small input errors have more time to be absorbed by the correction percentage adjustments.

## Iterative Use

If the block successfully delivers strength gains, it can be repeated until clear progress plateaus. Each successful completion provides updated 1RM inputs for the next iteration, with strength continuing to build across multiple blocks. The plateau signal is when 1RM tests after blocks no longer rise meaningfully — at that point, switching to a longer cycle (SRC2, SRC7) or a different block variant reintroduces variability.

Most lifters successfully iterate the block 2 to 4 times before plateau, accumulating substantial strength gains across the cumulative training window. Lifters who run the same block more than 4 times typically see diminishing returns and benefit from a different programming framework.

## Comparison: Block vs SRC7 for Peaking

Both the powerlifting block cycles and SRC7 serve peaking purposes. The block cycles are 4-week, level-graded across the full range from beginner to advanced. SRC7 is 12-week, calibrated specifically for Master of Sport to International Master of Sport-level athletes. The choice depends on the athlete's level and timing.

A Candidate to Master of Sport-level lifter with 4 weeks before a competition uses a block. A Master of Sport lifter with 12 weeks of preparation time uses SRC7 because the longer macrocycle allows more thorough peaking. A Master of Sport lifter with only 4 weeks before competition uses a block (advanced variant) because SRC7 cannot be compressed.

## When the Block Is the Wrong Choice

The block cycles are not appropriate for athletes whose primary need is base strength building. An athlete with a low absolute 1RM relative to body weight should run SRC1, SRC2, or SRC14 to build the base before switching to block-style peaking. Running a block on an undeveloped athlete produces a peak from a low base — useful for an immediate competition but not for long-term strength development.

The blocks are also not appropriate for athletes recovering from injury or coming off long layoffs. The intensive loading assumes the athlete is at or near their training-cycle peak readiness; restoration cycles or 12-week base cycles are the correct choice for athletes returning to training.
