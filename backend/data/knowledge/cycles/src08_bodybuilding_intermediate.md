# SRC8: Bodybuilding Mass-Gain Cycle (Intermediate)

## Overview

SRC8 is the LMS system's bodybuilding cycle for intermediate-level athletes. The 12-week program is oriented toward middle-level bodybuilders who have already trained systematically for one to two years while maintaining a sports regimen. The cycle's authors note explicitly that bodybuilding cycles haven't become standard practice in the LMS catalog the way powerlifting cycles have, because bodybuilding presumes an extremely individual approach with exercise selection accounting for individual strengths and weaknesses. SRC8 is the framework — individual customization of exercise choice remains the athlete's responsibility.

## Target Athlete Profile

Minimum intermediate experience level (1 to 2 years of consistent training). Body weight 80 kg or more — the cycle's loading assumptions are calibrated for athletes who are already meaningfully muscular. Lifters lighter than this should focus on building base strength before attempting hypertrophy specialization.

No severe muscular imbalances. The cycle's exercise distribution assumes a balanced base; athletes with major imbalances (e.g., undeveloped legs relative to upper body) need remediation work outside the cycle's structure. No acute joint injuries or movement disorders. The cycle is unsuitable for athletes with extremely demanding work schedules, because the volume requires consistent recovery capacity.

## Training Structure

Three training sessions per week. Optimal schedule is Monday-Wednesday-Friday or Tuesday-Thursday-Saturday so each session is followed by a recovery day. Twelve weeks, twelve microcycles.

The cycle's methodology uses high-repetition work with a minimum of eight reps per set — never fewer. This is the inverse of the strength-period rule (max six reps) used in powerlifting cycles. The 8+ rep minimum drives muscle protein synthesis and connective tissue adaptation in the hypertrophy range while keeping the neuromuscular load lower than strength work would.

The cycle is structured around movement patterns rather than lifts: a push-pattern day (chest, shoulders, triceps), a pull-pattern day (back, biceps, rear delts), and a leg day (quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves), with each day including direct work for the targeted muscle groups plus secondary work that reinforces the prior session's stimulus.

## Nutrition Guidance

The published instructions do not provide detailed macronutrient targets — bodybuilding nutrition is left to the individual athlete. The cycle's effectiveness depends on caloric surplus and adequate protein intake; an athlete who runs SRC8 in caloric deficit will see strength maintenance at best and likely some muscle loss. The general LMS guidance for hypertrophy phases applies: 1.6 to 2.2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight, caloric surplus of 200 to 500 kcal above maintenance, distribution of protein across four to five meals daily.

## Programming Parameters

The Excel file (cycle8.xlsm) contains the per-microcycle prescriptions. The athlete inputs personal maximums for the major compound lifts that anchor the cycle (squat, deadlift, bench, row patterns) and the spreadsheet calculates working weights for the 8+ rep range from those inputs.

## Implementation Steps

Verify athlete meets all conditions. Download cycle8.xlsm. Enable macros and editing. Review the exercise selection — and adapt where appropriate to the athlete's individual response history. The cycle's authors explicitly invite this adaptation rather than prescribing a fixed exercise list. Input 1RM values. Set training schedule. Begin.

## Why Bodybuilding Cycles Are Less Standardized

The LMS instructional text addresses this directly: bodybuilding presumes an extremely individual approach. Two athletes with the same body weight, training history, and starting strength can have substantially different exercise responses based on muscle architecture, lever lengths, and training history. SRC8 therefore functions more as a programming template than a prescriptive plan — the volume and intensity structure is fixed; the specific exercises within that structure should be customized.

This is the opposite of the powerlifting cycles, where the prescribed exercises (squat, bench, deadlift, and well-defined accessories) are universal because the sport's events are universal. Bodybuilding is judged on aesthetic outcomes, which depend on individual response patterns that templated programs cannot fully capture.
