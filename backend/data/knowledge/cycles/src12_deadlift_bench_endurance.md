# SRC12: Deadlift and Bench Endurance Cycle (Master of Sport to International Master of Sport)

## Overview

SRC12 is the elite-level deadlift-and-bench strength-endurance cycle in the LMS catalog. It is the endurance counterpart to SRC9 — same target athlete profile (Master of Sport to International Master of Sport lifters whose deadlift dominates), same lift focus (deadlift and bench, with squat de-emphasized), but with an 8+ rep range throughout instead of the strength-period 6-rep cap. The cycle is appropriate for elite deadlift specialists who need to expand work capacity and address muscular endurance during off-season preparation.

## Target Athlete Profile

Master of Sport or International Master of Sport level. Body weight 80 kg or more. Weight-to-height ratio should minimally deviate from recommended standards — significant deviations indicate the cycle's load calibration may be off. The deadlift must be the dominant movement, with significant superiority over the squat. Athletes with balanced or squat-dominant strength profiles should choose a different cycle.

No acute musculoskeletal injuries requiring immediate treatment. Adequate recovery capacity, low physical job demands. The cycle's volume accumulates over 12 weeks; insufficient recovery resources produce stalled progression rather than peak strength gains.

## Two Variants

SRC12 ships in two variants matching deadlift style. SRC12k (cycle12k.xlsm) is for classical (conventional) technique. SRC12s (cycle12s.xlsm) is for sumo. As with SRC9, the structural framework is identical; the deadlift variations and accessories differ to match each style's muscular demands.

The athlete commits to a variant for the full cycle. Switching styles mid-cycle invalidates the load math.

## Training Structure

Twelve consecutive weeks without competitions. Four sessions per week, optimally Monday-Tuesday-Thursday-Friday. The cycle is a strength-endurance period — repetition range is 8+ reps throughout (no fewer during warm-up or cool-down). This is the inverse of SRC9's six-rep cap.

Intensity is held at medium values throughout the cycle. The cycle does not use max-effort-style top singles; it uses sustained 8 to 12 rep work with occasional finishers in the 15 to 20 rep range.

## Rest Periods

Rest between heavy deadlift sets is extended — 6 to 10 minutes for full recovery. This is markedly longer than typical strength-cycle rests and reflects the cumulative fatigue cost of multiple sets of 8 to 12 reps on the deadlift. Insufficient rest produces form breakdown and accumulated lower-back stress; the long rest preserves form across the working volume.

## Programming Parameters

Correction percentage default is 0.5%, adjustable weekly. Pharmacology adjustments follow standard LMS rules.

The athlete inputs 1RM values for deadlift and bench press with 5 kg accuracy. Squat is not programmed; the athlete maintains it through their own supplementary work or accepts a temporary squat de-emphasis.

## Implementation Steps

Download the appropriate variant (cycle12k.xlsm or cycle12s.xlsm). Enable macros and editing. Verify technical competency for all prescribed exercises. Review cool-down recommendations in the first microcycle's comments. Input personal maxima for deadlift and bench press. Schedule four weekly training days. Adjust correction percentage weekly. Save and begin implementation.

## When to Use SRC12 vs SRC9

Both cycles target elite pull-dominant lifters. SRC9 is the strength-period cycle (6-rep cap, max-effort emphasis, peaking-style intensity waves). SRC12 is the endurance-period cycle (8+ reps, sustained moderate intensity, work capacity expansion). The natural sequence is to alternate: SRC12 builds work capacity and tissue adaptation during off-season, then SRC9 converts that capacity into peak strength as competition approaches.

Running only strength cycles produces athletes who plateau because their tissue capacity does not keep up with their strength. Running only endurance cycles produces athletes who do not express maximal strength because they never train the neuromuscular peak. The alternation is the productive pattern.

## Post-Cycle Progression

After SRC12, the recommended progression is back into strength work — SRC9 if the athlete is preparing for competition, or block cycles for powerlifters if the timing requires shorter peaking blocks. The 12 weeks of endurance work prepare the athlete for higher absolute intensities; without the endurance phase, those intensities would produce overuse and stagnation rather than progress.
