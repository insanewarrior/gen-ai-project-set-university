# SRC15: Bench Press Peak Cycle for Two Training Days per Week

## Overview

SRC15 is the LMS bench press cycle for athletes who can train only twice per week. The 12-week intensive cycle is designed to deliver peak strength outcomes despite the limited weekly frequency, making it appropriate for athletes whose work or life schedule prevents three-or-four-day training weeks but who still want serious bench press progress. Heavy loads dominate the cycle to extract maximum stimulus from each of the 24 total sessions across 12 weeks.

## Target Athlete Profile

Athletes aiming to progress specifically in bench press but limited to two training sessions per week. Body weight minimum 60 kg. Experience level II rank through Candidate to Master of Sport or equivalent results. The athlete must understand proper exercise technique and movement patterns — the cycle's heavy loading provides little tolerance for technical errors.

The cycle is suitable for athletes with demanding work schedules where additional training days are impractical, for those returning from time off who want to rebuild bench strength without overcommitting, and for athletes who choose to focus their gym time on bench press exclusively while devoting other physical activity to other goals.

## Training Structure

Two training days only per week. The two sessions must be spaced maximally apart — Monday and Thursday or Tuesday and Friday are the preferred patterns. Consecutive training days are explicitly avoided because the cycle's heavy loading requires full recovery between sessions, and back-to-back days prevent that.

Twelve weeks, twelve microcycles. No competitions or test attempts during the cycle.

The cycle is heavy-load-dominant. Heavy weights with fewer repetitions are prioritized over higher volume. The published instruction caps warm-up and preparatory work at six repetitions per set. The intensity profile across the 12 weeks builds toward peak strength readiness in week 13.

## Why Heavy Loads Suit Low-Frequency Training

When training frequency is low, each session must deliver substantial stimulus to compensate for the long inter-session recovery windows. High-rep endurance work fills those windows with adaptations the low frequency cannot recover from optimally. Heavy low-rep work, by contrast, produces neuromuscular gains that consolidate during the long recovery windows. This is why SRC15 is structured as a heavy peak cycle rather than as a high-volume cycle compressed into two days.

The dense per-session intensity also means warm-ups matter more in SRC15 than in higher-frequency cycles. Inadequate warm-ups under heavy loads at low frequency are a leading source of injury; the published instruction emphasizes warm-up importance explicitly.

## Setup Instructions (from the cycle15.xlsm spreadsheet)

Enable editing and macros when opening the file. Navigate to the "1 per day" sheet which contains the prescribed daily work. Input actual competition maxima for squat and bench press in the designated cells. Set the weekly percentage increase (correction percentage), with 0.5% recommended for natural athletes and a maximum of 2.5% under aggressive progression conditions. Adjust training dates and select the two weekdays for Sessions 1 and 2.

## Post-Workout Protocol

After completing all working sets, perform cool-down exercises at controlled tempo using moderate weights at 40 to 70% of maximum. Follow the cool-down with flexibility work emphasizing shoulder and chest mobility — the heavy bench loading produces shoulder tension that will accumulate without dedicated mobility work, eventually compromising both performance and joint health.

## Programming Parameters

Default correction percentage is 0.5% per microcycle. The maximum recommended for natural athletes is 2.5%, allowing aggressive progression for athletes who tolerate the load well. Pharmacology adjustments follow standard LMS rules.

## When to Pair SRC15 with SRC16

The LMS catalog pairs SRC15 (peak strength) and SRC16 (strength endurance) as complementary cycles for athletes limited to two training days per week. Running SRC15 followed by SRC16 produces a 24-week macrostructure that alternates peak strength with endurance, providing the variability needed to stimulate athlete growth across multiple periodization regimes despite the low weekly frequency. After 24 weeks, retest the maximum.

## Why SRC15 Differs from Higher-Frequency Cycles

SRC15 is not a smaller version of SRC5 or SRC6. The two-day-per-week structure changes which adaptations are available. Heavy low-frequency work emphasizes neural drive and connective tissue adaptation; the high-volume work that drives substantial hypertrophy in three-or-four-day cycles is impractical at two days because the per-session volume required would exceed recovery capacity. SRC15 leans into what the schedule allows rather than fighting it.

## Post-Cycle Progression

After SRC15, the recommended progression is SRC16 (endurance variant for two-day-per-week training) for variability, or a dedicated competition or max-attempt week (the cycle is configured to produce peak readiness in week 13). Athletes whose schedule changes to allow more training days can transition to SRC5 or SRC6 with updated 1RM inputs.
