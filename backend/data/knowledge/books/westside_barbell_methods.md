# The Westside Barbell Book of Methods (Louie Simmons, 2007)

## Origin

Louie Simmons wrote The Westside Barbell Book of Methods after thirty-six years of competitive powerlifting, two career-altering lower-back injuries, and a deep study of the Russian sports science literature translated by Bud Charniga. The book documents the system that made Westside Barbell — a small invitation-only gym in Columbus, Ohio — the most prolific producer of elite powerlifting totals in history. The system rejects American linear periodization in favor of the conjugate method, drawing on Vorobyev, Verkhoshansky, Zatsiorsky, and the Bulgarian weightlifting tradition.

Westside is closed to the public; about thirty members compete and roughly a third are sent to any given meet. The longevity of careers under this system is one of its quietly important benefits — most American lifters of the 1970s lasted five or six years; Westside members compete for decades.

## The Conjugate Method as a Whole

The conjugate method runs maximum effort, dynamic effort, and repeated effort training concurrently rather than in sequenced phases. The constraint that drives the structure: training above 90 percent of 1RM for more than three weeks weakens the central nervous system and reverses progress. The solution is to switch the maximum-effort movement every 1–3 weeks while keeping the load at 100 percent or above of that movement's 1RM. Westside lifters max out essentially every week of the year.

Zatsiorsky frames it: "Why climb three-quarters up the mountain only to go back down and start over?" Linear periodization climbs and descends. Conjugate keeps climbing by changing which face of the mountain is being climbed.

The four-day weekly structure is canonical: maximum effort squat/deadlift on Monday, maximum effort bench on Wednesday, dynamic effort squat/deadlift on Friday, dynamic effort bench on Sunday. 72 hours separates the two extreme workouts for the same lift. Each major day is followed by special-exercise accessory work targeting the lifter's specific weaknesses.

## Maximum Effort Day

Maximum effort builds absolute strength and strength-speed. The volume on max effort day consists of unidirectional loading — one workout contributes to the next. The exercises rotate but always target a 1–3 rep max with at least three sets at or above 90 percent.

For squat and deadlift, the rotation pulls from a deep catalog: low and high box squats with various specialty bars (cambered bar, safety squat bar, Manta Ray, front squat harness), good mornings (regular, suspended in chains, cambered bar, arch-back), deadlifts off pins, deadlifts standing on a platform, reverse band deadlifts. Westside very rarely pulls a competition deadlift in the gym — the goal is to strengthen the groove and the supporting musculature, not to fatigue the competition lift.

For bench press, the rotation: floor press (with or without chains), 2/3/4-board presses, incline press, reverse band press, rack lockouts at varying heights, cambered bar bench, illegal-wide grip bench (for 6–10 RM), bench with chains, high-rep dumbbell presses on flat or incline.

A key insight: bars other than a regular barbell are preferred for max effort lower-body work because they alter leverages in ways that stress the muscles harder than a competition lift would. The safety squat bar sits high on the neck and is constantly trying to throw the lifter forward — fighting that builds the upper back and stabilizers in exactly the way that prevents missed competition lifts where the lifter's chest drops.

## Dynamic Effort Day

Dynamic effort develops rate of force development and explosive strength. Sub-maximal loads are moved with maximum compensatory acceleration — the lifter applies as much force as if the bar were 100 percent loaded. For a 700-lb squatter training with 350 lbs, the goal is 700 lbs of force applied to that 350 lbs.

Bench dynamic uses 8–9 sets of 3 reps at 40–55 percent (advanced lifters use the lower end). Squat dynamic uses 10–12 sets of 2 reps at 50–60 percent. Deadlift dynamic, performed after dynamic squat, uses 6–10 sets of 1 rep at 60 percent.

The dynamic method is paired with accommodating resistance. Chains drape over the bar sleeve and partially deload at the bottom of the lift, matching the strength curve. Bands attach to the bottom of the rack, eliminate the deceleration phase, store kinetic energy on the eccentric, and add tension at lockout. Westside cycles three weeks of band work followed by three weeks of chain work to keep the contrast effect fresh.

## The Three Methods Combined: Reactive and Contrast

Several methods exploit the interaction between eccentric and concentric phases.

Box squatting trains static-overcome-by-dynamic and relaxed-overcome-by-dynamic work simultaneously. Sitting back (not down) on a box stretches the squatting muscles maximally; relaxing the hip flexors, glutes, and obliques on the box for a brief pause; then flexing dynamically off the box. The bar gains kinetic energy as it leaves the box, contributing to the concentric phase in a way that pause squats cannot.

Floor press works the same way for the bench. Lower until the elbows touch the floor, relax the triceps, then explode dynamically. Board press uses 2–3 stacked 2x6 boards on the chest for the same effect at a different range.

Weight releasers add eccentric overload (commonly 20 percent of 1RM hangs on the releaser, which falls off after the first rep) while the lifter only handles a sub-maximal concentric. This is a contrast method — the lifter reacts as if the heavier load were still on the bar.

The lightened method attaches bands to the top of the rack, deloading the bar at the bottom of the lift. With 250 lbs of band tension and 1000 lbs on the bar, the lifter unracks 1000, descends to 750 at the bottom, and reloads to 1000 at lockout. This trains acceleration through the entire range and teaches the lifter not to decelerate near completion.

The ballistic method on speed bench drops the bar fast and catches it 1–4 inches off the chest, reversing as fast as possible. Pausing on the chest in training is forbidden because kinetic energy is lost — the stretch reflex lasts up to four seconds in highly skilled lifters and two seconds in less-skilled athletes.

The heavy-light method uses bands then removes them mid-workout. Five sets of triples with two sets of mini-bands, then a set of bands removed for the remaining sets — the bar feels extremely light, and the body reacts as if both bands were still in place, increasing acceleration.

## Repeated Effort Method

The repetition method is used at Westside for special exercises (reverse hyper, belt squats, dumbbell work, sled drags) but never for the competition lifts themselves. The method runs in slow tempo (6–10 reps per minute, holding the bar between reps), targeting 100–200 reps total in some sets. It produces growth hormone elevation, lactic acid tolerance, and strength endurance.

Two intensity levels are used: one for strength (heavier sets, 30–60 percent) and one for restoration (under 30 percent). Restoration sets are not heavy enough to stop circulation, so they aid recovery rather than fatigue further. Vasili Alexeyev — the greatest Olympic lifter of all time — used a variation of this method, doing 2–3 minutes of nonstop power cleans and other hybrid exercises. He warmed up by throwing a 220-lb barbell over his head 100 times.

Sample repetition workouts: belt squats for 3–4 sets of 3 minutes, band good mornings for a single set lasting 6–8 minutes, walking with a sled for up to 5 minutes followed by abdominal work, dumbbell presses for 3–10 minutes with 40 to 100 lb dumbbells depending on the day.

## Special Exercises and Weak-Link Logic

The single most important programming decision is which special exercises to run. The principle: pick exercises that build YOUR particular weakness. Westside lifters individualize this aggressively — George Halbert's special exercises differ from Chuck Vogelpohl's, which differ from Amy Weisberger's.

For a weak lower back: back raises, straight-leg deadlifts off a platform, pull-throughs with straight legs, reverse hyperextensions. For weak hamstrings: heavy reverse hyperextensions, squatting pull-throughs, glute ham raises, sled pulling with hands behind the back. For weak glutes: heavy reverse hyperextensions, low belt squats, high-rep deadlifts (two sets of 20 reps, dropping the bar to just below the knees and catching/raising as fast as possible), glute ham raises. For weak abs: side bends, leg raises, standing lat machine curl-overs, strict sit-ups.

Pick one exercise per muscle group, push it hard until progress slows, then switch to the next. By rotating special exercises every 2–4 weeks as their effectiveness fades, the lifter raises strength continuously without the diminishing returns that come from sticking with any single accessory too long.

## The Reverse Hyperextension Machine

Louie invented the reverse hyper after a broken L5 vertebra in 1973 left him on and off crutches for ten months. The machine first relieved his pain, then rebuilt his back to its former strength. It's a foundational accessory at Westside for the deadlift and for spinal rehabilitation.

## Why Westside Avoids 80 Percent

Westside trains either very light (50 percent dynamic) or very heavy (100 percent or more on max effort day). Medium weights in the 80 to low-90 percent range are deliberately avoided because they're heavy enough to deplete the CNS but not heavy enough to drive maximum adaptation. Conventional Russian programming uses 80 percent extensively; Westside replaced that band with the conjugate-method rotation of max effort variations at 100 percent.

## Bands and Chains: The Mathematics of Accommodating Resistance

For speed-strength (dynamic effort), 65 percent of total weight should be barbell weight and 35 percent should come from band tension. For strength-speed used with maximal weights, the ratio inverts: 65 percent band tension, 35 percent barbell weight.

For squats, three bands are used: light bands (reducing load 200 lbs at the bottom of the squat, used over the Monolift), monster mini-bands (reducing 110 lbs), and mini-bands (reducing 55 lbs). Cycles run nine weeks: three weeks each with mini-bands, monster mini-bands, then light bands, with progressively heavier loads each microcycle. The strongest bands produce the greatest contrast.

For benching, four band strengths are used. The strong bands choked at the top of a 7-foot power rack reduce bar weight by 155 lbs at the chest. With 455 lbs on the bar, this means 300 at the chest progressively reloading to 455 at lockout. Medium bands reduce 95 lbs; light bands reduce 65 lbs.

## Westside Plateau-Breaking Templates

Tim Harold's lightened-method squat reached 1115 at the top and 1000 at the bottom against a best contest squat of 1005. Matt Smith reached 1150/1035 with the same method, producing an 1102 contest squat. The lightened method is used as one of the rotation choices on max effort day, not just as a contrast tool on dynamic day.

For deadlifting, a strong band looped over a pin 5'6" off the ground unloads the bar at floor level by about 135 lbs. Joe Bayles pulled 745 with this method, then 775 PR at a meet.

## Overall Programming Philosophy

The same pattern appears at every level: use all available training methods rather than rotating in and out of them; identify and aggressively attack weaknesses with rotating special exercises; max out every week of the year through movement variation rather than load periodization; and maintain dynamic and repeated effort work alongside max effort to preserve speed and work capacity. The system is harder to write down than linear periodization, but it produces the longest careers and the highest totals in the sport.
