# EFS Basic Training (Dave Tate and Jim Wendler, 2008)

## Origin and Purpose

EFS Basic Training is a 139-page training manual co-authored by Dave Tate and Jim Wendler in 2008, published through Elite Fitness Systems. It distills the conjugate method as practiced at Westside Barbell into a format meant to be read once and applied. The authors explicitly chose practical clarity over scientific verbosity, framing the book as an answer to the questions that recur most often at their seminars and Q&A sessions. Louie Simmons is credited as the source of the underlying method.

The book centers on a four-day weekly training split: max effort squat/deadlift on Monday, max effort bench press on Wednesday, dynamic effort squat/deadlift on Friday, dynamic effort bench press on Sunday. Each day follows a consistent structure of warm-up, main lift, supplemental movement, and accessory work. The 72-hour gap between max and dynamic days for the same lift is non-negotiable for recovery.

## Six Qualities of a Successful Training System

Dave and Jim identify six qualities that must coexist for any system to produce results: speed, strength, coaching, teamwork, attitude, and recovery. Most lifters obsess over the exercises and percentages but neglect the latter four. The illustrative argument: a 500-lb squatter dropped into Westside would almost certainly improve, not because the exercise selection is secret (it isn't — Louie has published it for decades), but because the coaching, teamwork, and accountability are present. These soft factors limit results far more often than program design.

A great coach has personal experience under the bar, motivates differently for different athletes, educates them in the "why" of every exercise, and is realistic about progress without being destructive about it. The example given: when an athlete benches 600 lbs, congratulate them, then remind them the gym is full of 600-lb benchers — the standard is higher.

## The Three Methods of Increasing Muscle Tension

The conjugate system trains all three methods every week. Max effort method is training at or above 90 percent of 1RM. Dynamic effort method is lifting a sub-maximal weight at the fastest possible speed (compensatory acceleration). Repetition method is lifting a sub-maximal weight to or near failure.

Western linear periodization fails because it sequences these methods rather than running them concurrently — when you stop the hypertrophy phase, you lose the gains; when you stop the dynamic phase, you lose the speed. The conjugate method maintains all three adaptations simultaneously by alternating which one is the focus of each weekly session.

## General Physical Preparedness

GPP is built through warm-ups that double as conditioning. Sled dragging is the staple — six trips of 200 feet with 30 seconds rest, walking forward, walking backward, and pulling face-pulls and rows attached to the sled. The sled's load stays light during warm-up; the goal is blood flow and weak-point work, not training stimulus.

The standard warm-up sequence after sled work: glute ham raises 3×8, hanging leg raises 3×12, push-ups 3×15. These are body-weight exercises chosen because they don't require a barbell, won't produce soreness, and don't tax the central nervous system. Recruits in military basic training do hundreds of push-ups daily and grow stronger without soreness — that's the model.

Conditioning beyond warm-ups uses walking (3–7 days per week, 20–40 minutes), walking with a 75-lb weight vest, stationary or outdoor bicycling, sled dragging at heavier loads (45 to 135 lbs for 10–20 minutes), the Prowler (used on training days, 10+ sprints of 30–50 yards), and medicine ball throws (20–30 minutes of throw-walk-pickup-throw cycles). The rule: conditioning should not interfere with strength training — if you get weaker in the gym or sore between workouts, the conditioning is too heavy.

## Max Effort Method Detail

Max effort develops the muscular and neural systems by reducing CNS inhibition and recruiting maximum motor units at optimal discharge frequency. The constraint: training above 90 percent for more than 1–3 weeks degrades the nervous system. The solution is exercise rotation — switch the max effort movement every 1–3 weeks (more advanced lifters rotate weekly; beginners can stay with one movement up to three weeks).

Max effort sessions follow this template: pick one multi-joint movement, warm up with sets of 3–5 reps starting from the bar, take small jumps (30 lbs for average lifters, 45–50 lbs for above-average), and work up to a 1RM or 3RM. Three sets should be at or above 90 percent. The session lasts 60–90 minutes after the warm-up.

The wrong way to progress (a common beginner pattern): jumping from 135 to 235 in four sets, totaling 1,030 lbs of work. The right way: progress through 45×5, 95×5, 115×5, 135×3, 155×3, 175×3, 195×2, 205×1, 220×1, 235×1, totaling 3,720 lbs — over three times the volume. The volume builds work capacity and prepares connective tissue for the heavy attempts.

Max effort lower-body movements rotate among three families. Box squats (low box 1–2 inches below parallel, high box 1–2 inches above parallel) overload the hips and back without the eccentric stress of a competition squat. Good mornings (often with the bar suspended in chains at waist height to eliminate the eccentric phase) develop the lower back, hamstrings, and glutes. Deadlift variations (rack pulls below the knee, deficit pulls, reverse band deadlifts, deadlifts against bands) train the pulling pattern without exhausting it with frequent competition-style maxes.

Max effort upper-body movements: floor press, 2/3/4-board press, incline press, reverse band press, rack lockouts, cambered bar bench, illegal-wide bench (for 6–10 RM rather than 1RM), bench with chains, high-rep dumbbell presses (every 4–5 weeks in place of the max-effort movement). Three sets at or above 90 percent is the rule.

## Beyond the Standard Max Effort

The book catalogs several variant max-effort methods. Multiple exertion is many singles at 70–80 percent with 2-minute rests — when the rest is short and the strain becomes high, it crosses from dynamic into max-effort territory. Maximal concentric uses pin pulls, pin presses, suspended squats, and other set-ups where the lifter only performs the concentric phase, allowing recovery from the eccentric damage that drives most muscle soreness. Maximal isometric uses pin presses against an empty bar held against the pin for 3–5 seconds at a sticking-point height, developing position-specific strength and reprogramming the lifter's mental response to that point in the lift.

Maximal eccentrics (130–140 percent loaded and lowered slowly) is acknowledged as effective but discouraged for intermediate and advanced lifters because the risk-to-benefit ratio is poor. Forced reps and reverse-band lightened-method work serve similar purposes more safely.

The maximal restricted-range method — rack presses, pin pulls, board presses, high box squats, partial leg presses, arch-back good mornings, overhead pin presses, pulling off stands — has been popular for thirty years for one reason: it works very well for overloading specific portions of the strength curve.

The circa-maximal method uses bands or chains to push the top of the lift above 90 percent while keeping the bottom manageable. Loading 50–60 percent bar weight plus heavy band tension produces a 1RM at the top that exceeds the 1RM possible with bar weight alone, training the lockout under conditions that recreate the heaviest portion of the strength curve without overstressing the bottom.

## Dynamic Effort Method Detail

Dynamic effort develops rate of force development. Bar weight is 50–70 percent of 1RM (Siff and Verkhoshansky cite two-thirds of 1RM as optimal for explosive strength), moved at maximum concentric speed. The athlete lowers under control then explodes upward as if the bar weighed 100 percent.

Bench press dynamic day prescribes 8 sets of 3 reps with 45–60 seconds rest. Three rotating grips: index finger just outside the smooth (close), thumb's-length from smooth (medium), pinky on the power ring (wide). Beginners use 60 percent. Intermediate lifters use 55 percent. Advanced lifters use 50 percent (some use 40–45 percent successfully — the percentage drops as the athlete becomes better at recruiting motor units).

Dynamic squat day uses 50–60 percent for 10–12 sets of 2 reps. Dynamic deadlift, performed after dynamic squat, uses 60 percent for 6–10 singles. Bar speed governs everything — the percentages are guidelines. If bar speed is maintained, increase the percentage; if it slows, decrease.

The dynamic method cycles in three forms: straight weight (or chains), bands, and combinations. Chains hang over the bar sleeve and partially deload at the bottom, accommodating the strength curve. Bands are stronger contrast — they eliminate the deceleration phase, store kinetic energy on the eccentric, and add tension at the lockout — but are harder on the body, so band cycles run only 3–4 weeks before swapping back to chains.

For benching with bands, subtract about 30 lbs from the usual chain-day bar weight. A mini-band always rides on dynamic bench press day.

## Bench Press Technique on Dynamic Day

Dynamic effort is the day to practice perfect form because the bar weight is light enough that nothing else is competing for attention. Six cues: shoulder blades pulled together and tight; pressure on the upper back and traps (test by sliding yourself back into the bench with leg drive — your eyes should end up directly under the bar); elbows tucked with the bar directly over wrists and elbows; bar low on the chest or upper abdomen; air filled into the belly (not the chest — shoulders should not rise on the inhale) and held; bar squeezed and pulled apart to activate the triceps.

To keep your butt on the bench at meets where the equipment is unfamiliar, three techniques: know your bench (test it before you lift), know your position (squeeze inner thighs into the bench or drive heels through shoulders depending on your setup), and Bill Gillespie's rope trick (a 5–10 lb plate suspended on a rope under the lifter's butt — if the lifter comes up, the plate falls). Five exercises that fix a chest sticking point: dumbbell bench, dumbbell incline, incline press, dumbbell floor press, floor press, military press, dumbbell military, illegal-wide bench, Bradford press, weighted dips. Pick one and do 4–6 sets of 10–15 reps.

## Why Conjugate Beats Linear Periodization

The Western 12–16 week cycle starting at 3×10 with 60 percent and ending with singles ignores the dynamic method entirely (the early-cycle reps are too high to apply force) and ignores the max effort method until the last few weeks. Two of the three force-production paths are absent until late in the cycle. The conjugate method addresses all three concurrently every week, producing strength gains that compound rather than rotating in and out of focus.

The cost is complexity. The conjugate method requires the lifter (or coach) to make weekly choices about which max effort variation to run, which accessories to prioritize, which contrast tools (bands, chains, releasers) to deploy. Linear periodization is easier to write down. But for athletes who have outgrown linear progression, the conjugate method is the standard answer.
